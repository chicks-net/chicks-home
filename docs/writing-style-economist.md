# The Economist Writing Style

Summarized from ["Writing with Style: The Economist Guide" by Lane Greene](https://education.economist.com/insights/what-to-read/writing-with-style)

## The Core Philosophy

**Write for the intelligent non-specialist.** Your reader is smart, busy, and
has no obligation to continue reading. Your job is to make every sentence earn
its place. Clarity is not a nicety — it is the entire point.

The Economist's style rests on three values: **clarity, brevity, and
precision**. If a choice must be made, clarity wins every time.

## Voice and Tone

- **Be confident.** Write declarative sentences. Hedging with "it could be
  argued that" or "one might say" signals weakness. If you believe something,
  say it.
- **Be authoritative without being pompous.** The tone is that of a
  knowledgeable friend explaining something over dinner, not a professor
  lecturing from a podium.
- **Avoid false modesty and false bravado.** State what you know; acknowledge
  what you don't.
- **No throat-clearing.** Don't warm up the engine. Start with the substance.
- **Wit is welcome; cleverness for its own sake is not.** A well-placed aside
  or turn of phrase earns its keep; puns that require effort to parse do not.

## Sentences and Structure

- **Prefer short sentences.** When a sentence requires a second reading, it has
  failed. Break it up.
- **Use the active voice by default.** Passive voice has its uses (when the
  agent is unknown or unimportant), but reach for the active unless there's a
  specific reason not to.
- **Put the strong word at the end.** Sentences lose energy when they trail off
  into weak qualifiers. The most powerful word belongs at the period.
- **Vary sentence length.** A string of identically structured sentences
  creates monotony. Short after long provides rhythm. Like this.
- **One idea per paragraph.** The paragraph break is a signal to the reader.
  Use it honestly.
- **Lead with the news.** In any piece, the most important thing comes first —
  not buried in paragraph four after extensive scene-setting.

## Words

- **Prefer concrete to abstract.** "Rising costs hurt consumers" beats
  "inflationary pressures negatively impact purchasing power." Show the thing,
  not a category of the thing.
- **Use strong verbs.** Verbs carry sentences. Avoid turning verbs into nouns
  ("make a decision" → "decide"; "reach a conclusion" → "conclude").
- **Cut adverbs ruthlessly.** If the verb is right, the adverb is usually
  redundant. "He said firmly" — either the quote is firm or it isn't.
- **No weasel words.** "Some say," "many believe," "experts argue" without
  attribution is lazy and evasive. Who says? Which experts?
- **No clichés.** When you reach for a familiar phrase, stop and find a fresher
  one. Clichés signal autopilot writing.
- **No jargon without explanation.** Technical terms are fine when necessary —
  briefly define them on first use, then use them consistently.
- **Avoid euphemisms.** Call things what they are. "Fired" not "let go."
  "Cheap" not "affordable."
- **Be specific with numbers.** "A majority" means nothing. "63%" means
  something. Prefer the latter.

## Economy

- **If a word does no work, cut it.** Read every sentence and ask what each
  word contributes. "Currently" is almost always redundant ("currently is" →
  "is"). "Very" weakens more than it strengthens.
- **Don't say what you're about to say.** "It is worth noting that X" — just
  say X.
- **Don't recap what you just said.** Summaries within an article are a sign of
  lost confidence.
- **Avoid throat-clearing transitions.** "Having established X, we can now turn
  to Y" — just turn to Y.

## Structure and Argument

- **Every piece needs a reason to exist now** (the "peg"). What happened? Why
  does this matter today? If you can't answer that, you don't have a piece yet.
- **Tell the reader what they'll learn, then teach it, but don't tell them you
  taught it.** The three-part structure (tell/show/summarize) works in
  speeches; in writing, the summary is usually dead weight.
- **Build to a conclusion, don't just stop.** The ending should feel earned — a
  synthesis, an implication, or a lingering question. Not a restatement of the
  lede.
- **Transitions should be invisible.** The end of one paragraph should pull the
  reader into the next naturally. If you're writing "Furthermore..." or "In
  conclusion...", find a better link.

## Common Traps to Avoid

| Trap | Fix |
| --- | --- |
| "The fact that" | Delete it; restructure |
| "In order to" | Replace with "to" |
| "Due to the fact that" | Replace with "because" |
| "At this point in time" | Replace with "now" |
| "It is important to note" | Just say the important thing |
| Passive voice pileup | Identify the actor; make them the subject |
| Scare quotes | Use them rarely; they suggest you don't believe |
| Noun strings | Unpack into a real sentence |

## On Research and Honesty

- **Show your sources, implicitly or explicitly.** Even without footnotes, good
  writing signals where claims come from.
- **Distinguish fact from opinion clearly.** Don't let analysis masquerade as
  reporting or vice versa.
- **Acknowledge complexity without hiding behind it.** "It's complicated" is
  never an ending; it's a starting point.
- **Don't overstate your conclusion.** If the evidence is suggestive, say
  suggestive. Certainty you haven't earned will cost you credibility.

## For Technical Writing (Applied to Chicks.net)

The Economist style translates directly to technical blogging:

- **Lead with the problem, not the solution.** Readers need to recognize their
  situation before they'll care about your fix.
- **Name the tool/concept early.** Don't build suspense around what you're
  writing about; get to the noun.
- **Use examples as your primary explanatory device.** Code blocks, commands,
  and config snippets are your concrete nouns. Let them carry the argument.
- **Don't over-explain what the reader already knows.** Trust your audience. A
  reader who arrived at a post about DNSControl already knows what DNS is.
- **Explain your reasoning, not just your steps.** "I did X because Y" is more
  useful than "I did X."
- **Write the takeaway as the title.** "How I Migrated DNS Records with
  DNSControl" is better than "My DNS Migration Journey."
